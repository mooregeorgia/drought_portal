Aim


To design a new Asia-Pacific Sub-seasonal to Seasonal (S2S) Prediction Portal to provide the National Meteorological and Hydrological Services (NMHSs) in the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) Regions II (Asia) and V (South-West Pacific) with the Australian Community Climate Earth-System Simulator – Seasonal (ACCESS-S) S2S climate forecasts as well as to the WMO Space-based Weather and Climate Extremes Monitoring (SWCEM) precipitation estimate and derived products.

This project has been conducted in order to strengthen the capacity for Sub-seasonal to Seasonal Prediction Services in Asia-Pacific Countries. Which aims to design a new Asia-Pacific Sub-seasonal to Seasonal (S2S) Prediction Portal to provide the National Meteorological and Hydrological Services (NMHSs) in the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) Regions II (Asia) and V (South-West Pacific) with the Australian Community Climate Earth-System Simulator – Seasonal (ACCESS-S) S2S climate forecasts as well as to the WMO Space-based Weather and Climate Extremes Monitoring (SWCEM) precipitation estimate and derived products.




About


Seasonal and inter-annual climate variability is a major factor in determining the vulnerability of many countries in Asia and the Pacific to climate change, particularly due to the large impact of the El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO). Many of the earliest impacts of climate change are felt with the interaction of variability and change, giving rise to events outside of the past historical records. One effective and low cost option to reduce vulnerability to climate change is to improve the accuracy, availability and use of sub-seasonal to seasonal (S2S) forecasts which is the primary motivation of this project.


To provide reliable seasonal prediction service, the Australian Bureau of Meteorology (the Bureau) has developed a dynamical seasonal forecast system POAMA (Predictive Ocean Atmosphere Model for Australia) based on a coupled model of the ocean and atmosphere. The model demonstrated good skill at predicting El Niño and La Niña up to 9 months in advance. In 2012, developement and deployement of seasonal climate predictions based upon POAMA providing Meteorological Agencies in Asia-Pacific with an additional tool to analyse sea surface temperatures, air temperature, pressure and rainfall and prepare more accurate seasonal climate predictions for the future.
